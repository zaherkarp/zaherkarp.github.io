## Camp critiques

## Critique from Encoding rigorists

### Voicing critics
Edward Tufte, Jacques Bertin, Tamara Munzner, Claus Wilke

### What works on this page (your camp's lens only)
- The combined Education/Service gantt (`figure.gantt-figure`, lines 2576-2681) is the page's strongest encoding: a shared year axis, two semantically named lanes, explicit distinction between durational (bar) and instantaneous (square) marks, with the 2014-15 cluster annotation pointing at structure the prose actively hides. Position-along-axis is doing the work, Bertin's strongest channel, and the figure earns its space by revealing concurrency.
- The cliff-figure's deliberate refusal to label the y-axis (lines 2027-2031 in comments, executed at lines 2068-2079) is correct: density on an approximate bimodal sketch shouldn't invite numerical reading. That restraint is what the comment claims and what the chart delivers.

### What needs to change (your camp's lens only)

1. **Cliff figure (`figure.cliff-figure`, lines 2032-2120) is encoding-fraud, not a density estimate.** The bezier path at lines 2068-2079 is hand-drawn ("smooth bezier approximating") with no data, no estimator, no bandwidth, no source — yet it is presented in a figcaption as "Approximate distribution of Medicare Advantage contract Star Ratings." Approximate means "computed from the real distribution and rounded"; this is decorative. The +$50M annotation (line 2108) attaches a hard dollar claim to a shape that is not a measurement. Fix: either render an actual CMS contract-rating histogram (CMS publishes the distribution), or relabel the figure honestly as "Schematic illustration" and remove the +$50M annotation pinned to a fictional density peak. Uncertainty must be visible; here it is invisible because there is no data underneath.

2. **Citation-count dots use area-encoded magnitude with no scale and only four discrete values (lines 1546-1562, 1672-1683).** The mapping `unknown=4, 4 cites=5, 11 cites=7, 23 cites=10` (mobile: 2.5/3/4/5.5) is a non-monotonic, non-linear, hand-picked lookup, and worst of all "unknown" is encoded as a value (radius 4) that collides with "4 citations" (radius 5). Munzner: when an attribute is missing, do not silently map it to the lowest non-zero magnitude — the reader cannot tell a zero-citation paper from a four-citation paper at this scale, and visually a missing measurement is indistinguishable from a small one. Fix: drop dot-area encoding entirely for n=6 publications. Use a lollipop/dot-plot with citation count on a real numeric y-axis, or print the integer next to each dot. With six observations, the table is the chart.

3. **Sankey/slope figure (`figure.sankey-figure`, lines 2166-2253) advertises illustrative shares while encoding them with stroke-width — the perceptually weakest length-like channel for ratio comparison.** Worse, the bars at x=360 use height to encode the same share (lines 2219, 2225, 2232, 2239, 2248), so the reader is asked to judge identical quantities via two different visual channels (line thickness AND bar height) for the same data. Bertin/Munzner: don't double-encode and don't double-encode badly. The "10 candidates" Health Info Tech bar (height 20) is half the height of the "RN" bar (50) for a 25-vs-10 ratio that should be 2.5:1 — the encoding is wrong, not just weak. Fix: pick one channel (bar length on a shared baseline), label every value, drop the connecting lines, and stop calling this a Sankey.

4. **Career arc (lines 1284-1363) flattens duration into rank-order bars with no consistent encoding of role intensity, scope, or seniority — only start/end.** Three lanes look like categorical small multiples, but rows 1-3 (writing, research, data eng) are not comparable categories; they are a chronology forced into parallel rows. The 2020 acquisition gets accent treatment (lines 1334-1336) while the equally consequential 2017 entry into data engineering gets none, even though that transition is the central narrative the chart purports to show. Fix: either make the lanes truly comparable (e.g., all three on the same "role" scale with a shared duration encoding), or collapse to a single timeline ribbon with multiple labeled inflection points so the reader doesn't infer a categorical equivalence between "Editorial" and "Data engineering" that the data doesn't support.

5. **Writing cadence sparkline (lines 1747-1773) lies about its own pattern.** 23 of 24 dots are identical filled circles (binary "post"/"no post"), one is muted (line 1765), and the trailing "31 posts" overcounts what the dots can represent (24 weeks cannot contain 31 posts at 1/dot). The sparkline encoding promises week-by-week cadence; the data showing six posts in three days (the entries at lines 1779-1825 list four posts on 2026-05-19 alone) cannot be expressed by one dot per week. Fix: either show per-week post counts as a true sparkline (stem/bar heights), or drop the visualization and print the cadence claim as text. The current chart is decorative.

6. **Accent-color encoding contract is inconsistent across figures.** The career arc uses red for one acquisition event (line 1334). The cliff figure uses red for both the threshold line and the dollar annotation (lines 2098, 2101, 2108). The 2014 highest-cited publication in the dot plot gets no accent at all (line 1551) — instead a muted gray "most-cited" label (line 1608) — even though that's exactly the kind of inflection the rationale doc says accent should carry. CLAUDE.md says one or two accent uses per chart; the rule needs to mean something or be dropped. Fix: declare what semantic role red plays (e.g., "single most consequential pivot point") and apply it consistently across all six figures, or remove accent from the cliff figure's $50M label since the dashed line already carries the callout.

7. **The dot-plot's mobile version (lines 1649-1704) silently changes the encoding between viewports.** Desktop stacks dots vertically (countable); mobile uses bar height (continuous magnitude). The figcaption (line 1509) claims the chart shows "presentations" — but at one breakpoint a reader counts seven dots and at the other estimates a bar's pixel height. Munzner: the chart's claim must survive the viewport. Fix: keep the encoding constant (dots both places, scaled down) or change the claim. The comment at line 1632 admits seven stacked dots "become illegible smudges" — that's evidence the encoding is wrong, not that the viewport needs a different chart.

### What other camps will say that you disagree with

- **Editorial clarity** will argue the cliff curve is "good enough as a teaching figure" and the +$50M label is the whole point of the chart. We reject this: a teaching figure that fabricates the shape it teaches *with* is not a teaching figure, it is a billboard. Replace it with the real distribution or label it schematic.
- **Data humanists** will defend the career arc's red 2020 callout as emotionally accurate, the page's "human" inflection point. We reject this: emotional accuracy is not a license to assign perceptual rank inconsistently across figures. If red means "career pivot," it must be applied to 2017 too.
- **System designers** will say the dot-area encoding on publications is fine because it's just a thumbnail of activity. We reject this: thumbnails still encode, and a thumbnail that collides "unknown" with "small" makes the reader misread the corpus. Six points fit in a table.
- **Access floor** will claim the cliff figure's hatch pattern is an inclusion win for non-color-perceiving readers. We agree on the hatch, but the hatch can't rescue a curve that has no underlying data; accessibility of a wrong encoding is still a wrong encoding.

### Your camp's hill to die on

The cliff figure (lines 2032-2120) must either be redrawn from the actual CMS contract-rating distribution or relabeled as a schematic illustration with the +$50M annotation removed. This is foundational, not contextual, because the page's entire credibility argument rests on the claim that Z translates regulatory measurement into auditable analytics, and the most prominent figure in the most prominent project is a hand-drawn bezier presented in its caption as an "approximate distribution." Decoration dressed as estimation is precisely the failure mode our camp exists to refuse; once we accept it here, every other chart on the page becomes deniably aesthetic too. The CMS Star Ratings public-use data file contains the contract-level rating distribution. Use it.

## Critique from Editorial clarity

### Voicing critics
Amanda Cox, John Burn-Murdoch, Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic, Hannah Ritchie, Andy Kirk, Lisa Charlotte Muth, Alberto Cairo

### What works on this page (your camp's lens only)
- The Stars cliff figure (lines 2037-2119) earns its keep: one frame, one takeaway, direct red label ("+$50M") next to the dashed line, no legend lookup required. This is exactly the Nussbaumer "what do you want them to know" discipline applied — the chart and its caption agree on a single sentence.
- The 2020 acquisition callout on the career arc (lines 1334-1336) is the right kind of annotation: the eye lands on the red, the prose under the chart never has to reintroduce the year. Burn-Murdoch's "let the chart do the sentence" move.

### What needs to change (your camp's lens only)

1. **Subtitle (line 1253) buries the takeaway.** "Healthcare data engineering and Medicare Advantage analytics" describes a category, not a claim. The reader's first three seconds need a what-he-actually-does sentence with a denominator or a verb. Fix: rewrite to a one-line claim like "Builds Medicare Star Ratings and HEDIS pipelines for plans covering [N] million members" — something a recruiter can repeat in a Slack message.

2. **Academic activity dot plot (figcaption line 1508-1510, SVG 1512-1612) has no takeaway and three competing ones.** The figcaption announces 6 publications and 17 presentations; the chart annotates "most-cited" and "Patient Choice Award." That's three signals fighting for the eye. Cox's question: what's the one thing? If the answer is "an intense 2014-2015 burst that ended when industry started" — say that in the caption and in a single direct label on 2015's tall column. Drop the "most-cited" floating annotation; the dot is already largest. Drop "Patient Choice Award" — it belongs in Speaking, not on a frequency chart.

3. **The "31 posts" cadence sparkline (lines 1744-1776) hides its finding inside a sidenote.** The interesting fact is the late-2025 return-to-writing and the recent weekly cadence; the chart shows a single near-gap dot at week 17 and otherwise reads as a uniform run. Muth's principle: if your tag-frequency breakdown is the actual content, do not gate it behind a ⊕ toggle on the homepage; demote the bar of dots to a true sparkline (vary fill density by post count per week, not 0/1) so the cadence *shape* carries the story without the toggle.

4. **Gantt density annotation (line 2679) tells, the chart does not show.** "2014-15: three credentials, two roles" is a caption-as-confession that the visual didn't land. Ritchie's standard: if the reader cannot read the cluster off the geometry, the geometry needs work — tighten the lane height, drop a faint vertical band across the 2014-15 columns, then remove the prose annotation. Right now the bars are 4px tall on a 320-unit canvas and the eye reads them as scattered dashes, not a cluster.

5. **The two H2 subtitles "Featured" (line 1996) and "More projects" (line 2283) are chrome that introduces nothing.** Kirk would cut them. The visual difference between a 60%-column featured card with a figure and a 4-column tile grid already communicates tier. Adding italic muted labels above each is the dataviz equivalent of putting "Chart:" above a chart. Delete both; trust the layout.

6. **Cliff figure caption (line 2034) under-claims.** "Approximate distribution... showing the 4.0 QBP threshold" describes the *figure*, not the *finding*. Cairo: the caption should be the sentence you'd say aloud pointing at the chart. Rewrite to: "Most Medicare Advantage contracts cluster just below the 4.0 cutoff that triggers $50M in QBP bonus payments." Now the chart and caption work as one editorial unit.

7. **Sankey-style transition figure (figcaption line 2167-2169, lines 2171-2252) labels everything but tells nothing.** Five target occupations with three-line subtitles each ("25 candidates, ADN or BSN program") is a table dressed as a chart. The one editorial idea — that most Medical Assistants are *trainable* with short certifications, not stuck — is buried in the bar geometry. Direct-label the 60% Trainable share visibly, demote the four candidate-count subtitles into the figcaption or the project prose, and let the slope-graph carry shape, not metadata.

### What other camps will say that you disagree with

- **Encoding rigorists** will defend the cliff-figure's unlabeled y-axis (line 2027-2030 comment claims "showing axis numbers would invite spurious precision"). We disagree mildly: the reader should at least be told the y-axis is "density of contracts" via an axis title; "deliberately unlabeled" is a rigor pose that costs comprehension.
- **Data humanists** will defend the Patient Choice Award and "most-cited" annotations on the dot plot as personal warmth. We disagree: warmth on a frequency chart is decoration the eye has to parse around; put those in prose where they read as voice, not data.
- **System designers** will defend the "Featured" / "More projects" subhead labels as wayfinding. We disagree: good layout is its own wayfinding, and adding italic chrome to compensate for layout uncertainty is a tell that the layout isn't pulling its weight.
- **Pipeline and reproducibility** will defend the tag-frequency margin note (line 1774) as honest disclosure of what `build_portfolio.py` knows. We disagree: a 36-tag pile-of-counts is a data dump, not a finding; pipelines exist to serve the reader, not document themselves.

### Your camp's hill to die on

Every figure on this page must have a one-sentence takeaway that the figcaption states plainly and the geometry shows without help. Right now four of six figures (career arc, dot plot, gantt, sankey) have captions that describe what the figure *is* rather than what it *means*, and at least two (gantt density cluster, sankey 60% trainable share) bury their finding inside the encoding. The rewrite is small and unglamorous — twelve to twenty words per figcaption, one direct-label move per chart, the deletion of a few decorative annotations — but it is the difference between a portfolio that *shows* data and one that *argues* with data. A page this typographically considered should not let the reader do the editorial work the author skipped; the whole Tufte lineage this site invokes was built on the premise that the writer and the chart speak with one voice.

## Critique from System designers

### Voicing critics
Ben Shneiderman, Don Norman, Steve Krug, Khoi Vinh, John Maeda, Moritz Stefaner, Shirley Wu, Mike Bostock, Jeffrey Heer

### What works on this page (your camp's lens only)
- The CSS-checkbox sidenote/marginnote system (lines 290-401, with the focus-projection trick at line 348-352) is a real interaction: pure-CSS toggling, keyboard-reachable, mobile-collapse behavior. Shneiderman and Norman both nod: state is real, affordance is reasonable, focus is preserved without JS. The "View all writing" link at line 1828 is a correct overview-to-detail handoff for the Writing list.

### What needs to change (your camp's lens only)
- **Mobile sidenote/marginnote toggle has no visible affordance for what is hidden** (lines 376-401, all marginnote `&#8853;` labels page-wide). On narrow viewports, the only visible cue is a small `⊕` glyph or a superscript numeral with a thin grey underline. Krug and Norman: a control whose result is unguessable fails the "don't make me think" rule. Fix: when a marginnote is collapsed on mobile, render the toggle as text like "tag breakdown" or "PubMed link", not a symbol; the symbol is decoration, not a label. The numbered sidenote can stay numeric but should display the first 5-7 words of the note's body as a peekable preview on tap-hold or as inline preceding text. The current pattern is "click this dot to reveal something" which is the antipattern Heer and Bostock call "mystery meat."

- **The career arc and academic dot plot are frozen images with no interaction whatsoever** (lines 1284-1466 horizontal/vertical career SVG; lines 1512-1704 dot-plot SVGs). The whole page is a static portrait. Bostock/Stefaner/Heer: every dot in the publications row is a citation count tied to a real paper listed lower on the page in `<section id="publications">` (line 2345). Those dots should be `<a xlink:href="#mn-jihi">` or similar, so clicking the 2014 dot scrolls to and highlights the JIHI publication entry. The 2020 acquisition red dot should link to the Health Catalyst experience block. Right now the chart is illustrative, not operative. This is the "charts treated as islands" failure mode our camp explicitly demotes. Earned, lightweight interaction: anchor-link the dots; no library, no JS framework, just `<a>` wrappers around `<circle>`.

- **Nav has nine items and no current-section indicator** (lines 1228-1240). Vinh and Krug: nine flat items wrapping at medium widths is a legibility problem, but the worse problem is no in-page wayfinding. Reading the page at 2,800 lines worth of content, a user has no idea where they are. Fix: add a `:target` rule or a scrollspy via `IntersectionObserver` (you allow this kind of interaction-is-the-point exception elsewhere in the codebase, so it's defensible) that bolds the current nav item. Alternative within the no-JS rule: add a section-counter label ("about · 1 of 9") to the nav at minimum. Hierarchy without orientation is just a list.

- **Speaking section's `<details>` collapses 17 conferences with no preview or filtering** (lines 2468-2489). Shneiderman's mantra: overview first, then zoom and filter. The reader gets neither overview (the dot plot shows raw counts per year but doesn't connect to this list) nor filter (no year filter, no venue-category facet, no search). Fix at minimum: show the top three most-prestigious or most-recent talks above the fold of the `<details>`, then collapse the remainder. Better: have the dot-plot presentation dots above (lines 1571-1600) link to the corresponding `<li>` in the speaking list. Right now the same data is rendered twice, in two different sections, with zero linkage between them.

- **The 4.0 cliff figure (lines 2032-2120) is the literal subject of the first featured project, which IS an interactive simulator, yet the figure is a static SVG with no "try it" affordance attached** (line 2098 dashed cliff line). Bret would die on this hill. Heer and Bostock: this is the place where a 50-line vanilla-JS proof-of-concept (a draggable cliff line that updates the `+$50M` label) would be defensible under your blog-experiment-subpage exception, OR at minimum the dashed red line itself should be wrapped in `<a href="/star-rating-predictor/">` so clicking the cliff in the figure goes to the simulator. The "Live demo" text link below at line 2123 is buried beneath the figure; the figure should BE the affordance. Fix: wrap the cliff line + `+$50M` label in an anchor to the simulator; add `cursor: pointer` on hover. One affordance, one CSS rule, no JS.

- **Sidenote toggles use checkbox state but never confirm to the user that anything changed on desktop** (lines 290-302, 367-368). On wide screens the marginnote is always visible in the right margin, so the toggle is a no-op. Norman: a control that does nothing visible when activated is a broken control. Either hide the marginnote on desktop too and require the toggle to reveal it (consistent interaction across viewport sizes), or make the toggle label become invisible/inert at wide widths so the user isn't presented with a control that doesn't do anything. The current state is: tapping `⊕` on desktop appears to do nothing because the note was already in the margin.

- **The "Healthcare Workforce Transition Platform" slope-graph (lines 2166-2253) shows static counts for ONE worked example (Medical Assistant → 5 targets)** with no way to inspect another source occupation, even though the project description (line 2135) emphasizes the system's generalizability across "clinical and administrative roles." Stefaner and Wu: this is a system being represented by a single frozen frame. Minimum fix: add a small `<select>` or radio group above the chart letting the reader pick from 3-4 source occupations (Medical Assistant, Medical Secretary, Pharmacy Tech), with the bars and stroke widths updating via CSS-only `:checked` state and pre-computed SVG groups. This falls within your existing "interactivity is the whole point" lane on the simulator page; the slope graph is currently making a claim ("the platform works across roles") that the visual evidence ("here's one role") doesn't support.

### What other camps will say that you disagree with
- **Encoding rigorists** will say the dot plot's citation-radius mapping (radius = 4/5/7/10 at lines 1548-1562) doesn't preserve area-proportionality and should be fixed. We disagree: chart accuracy matters less than chart operability; we'd rather link the dots than rescale them.
- **Editorial clarity** will say nav is fine because it's restrained typography and the small-caps work. We disagree: typography isn't navigation; nine items without a "you are here" signal is editorial calm masking a usability failure.
- **Access floor** will defend the `⊕` glyph and the existing focus-ring projection (line 348). We disagree partially: keyboard reachability is necessary but not sufficient. A reachable control that doesn't communicate what it reveals (the `⊕` label) still fails Norman's gulf-of-evaluation test, for keyboard users especially.
- **Pipeline and reproducibility** will defend the static SVGs because they're CI-generated, em-dash-linted, and trivially diffable. We disagree: build hygiene is not user value; a chart that builds reliably but doesn't let the reader operate on it is a beautifully reproducible dead-end.

### Your camp's hill to die on
**The publication and presentation dots in the academic activity figure (lines 1538-1611, 1678-1705) MUST become clickable links to their corresponding entries in the Publications and Speaking sections.** Not "should" — must. This is foundational because every other system-design concern on this page (nav wayfinding, simulator-as-affordance, slope-graph generalizability) is downstream of one principle: a visualization on a portfolio page where the same data already exists as prose below is a system, not a picture. The page currently shows you the shape of the data and then asks you to scroll, scan, and manually match the 2014 standout dot to the JIHI paper. That's a coordination failure between two views of the same dataset, and Shneiderman's overview-zoom-filter triad collapses to overview-only without this link. The fix is roughly 12 lines of SVG markup (wrap each `<circle>` in an `<a xlink:href="#mn-jihi">`, etc.), zero JavaScript, zero new dependencies, and it converts the figure from a thumbnail into an index. Without it, the chart is decoration with a citation count; with it, the chart is the interface to the section beneath.

### What works on this page (your camp's lens only)
