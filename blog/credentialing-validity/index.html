Credentialing Looks Rigorous. Its Performance Scores Are Mostly Noise.

June 26, 2026 78 min read
  • healthcare
  • credentialing
  • statistics
  • reliability

A system built to reassure

Physician credentialing and privileging are built to reassure. A hospital verifies a license, confirms a board certification, reviews a work history, and then keeps watch through performance data. The apparatus has high face validity. It looks like exactly what a careful institution would do to keep a dangerous provider away from patients, and that resemblance is most of why we trust it. This piece is about a different property, predictive validity, which is whether the same apparatus actually separates a safe provider from a dangerous one. The two are not the same thing, and the distance between them is statistical, not moral or procedural.

Consider the ongoing-monitoring half of the system. The Joint Commission requires Ongoing Professional Practice Evaluation, or OPPE: performance data collected on each individual privileged provider and factored into the decision to keep, limit, or revoke privileges. The intent is sound and the data are real. The difficulty is arithmetic. A dangerous provider is a rare event. Any one physician accrues only a small number of relevant cases in a year. A rare numerator measured over a small denominator produces an estimate that is dominated by noise rather than by the quantity you wanted to measure, and no amount of administrative care around the edges changes that core.

Reliability is signal divided by noise

The standard way to make this precise is the reliability of a physician-level measure, the share of the observed variation between physicians that reflects true differences rather than measurement error. Written out, it is R(n) = var_between / (var_between + var_within / n). Reliability rises with genuine physician-to-physician variation and falls as measurement noise grows, and because the noise term shrinks as one over the caseload, sample size is what tips the balance. The chart below traces that curve. At realistic case volumes the measure barely lifts off the floor.

With the illustrative variance values shown, a physician with thirty cases has a reliability near 0.40, and across a twenty-to-fifty-case band it stays between 0.31 and 0.53, far below any threshold one would trust for a decision about someone's privileges. The published literature lands in the same place from the other direction. In a study of physician-level patient-experience measures, reaching a reliability of 0.90 required between 138 and 255 patients per physician once the adjustment was done carefully, and a change from standard to expanded risk adjustment reclassified 8 of 56 doctors across the top and bottom quartiles, about one in seven. Patient experience is easier to measure than rare clinical harm, so if anything that is the optimistic end of the problem.

What pure noise looks like when you rank it

It helps to see what a noise-dominated measure does when you rank people with it. The cleanest way to see it is to build a world with no real differences at all and watch the ranking invent some. In the funnel plot below, every one of three hundred simulated providers shares a single true event rate of three percent. Their caseloads are drawn from a skewed distribution with a median near thirty, so most carry few cases and a few carry many, and the only thing that varies between them is luck. The control limits are computed, not sketched: they are the true rate plus or minus z times the binomial standard error, sqrt(p0 (1 - p0) / n), evaluated across the range of volumes.

Nearly every provider falls inside the funnel, exactly as they should, because they are in truth identical. Yet a naive worst-to-best ranking still lifts a handful above the line and labels them outliers. In this run 15 providers cross the ninety-five-percent limit despite sharing the same true rate. That is more than the one in forty a ninety-five-percent upper limit nominally allows, because the normal-approximation control limits are anti-conservative when events are this rare and caseloads this small, the same arithmetic the rest of this piece is about. This is the league-table fallacy: the spread is noise, and ranking is a machine for converting noise into apparent signal. A committee handed the sorted list would have fifteen names to explain, and every one of them is a coin that came up heads a few too many times.

The ranking does not survive a second year

If the ranking were measuring something real, it would hold still from one year to the next. It does not. Drawing two independent years from the identical true rate and sorting each into quartiles produces the slopegraph below.

Because the data were built with no true differences whatsoever, every line that changes height is pure noise, and 68 percent of providers land in a different quartile the second year. Set that against the one-in-seven reclassification the patient-experience study found between two adjustment methods on real data. The simulation churns more, not less, because resampling the same providers at low volume is a harsher test than a single change of method. Either way the lesson is the same: a quartile label that reshuffles this freely is not a measurement of the provider, it is a measurement of the year.

The yield, in dollars

None of this is hypothetical at the level of yield. In a 2023 study in the Journal of the American College of Radiology, six hospital systems totaling 12,854 privileged providers found that zero were flagged as outliers through the OPPE metrics alone, at a recurring labor cost near fifty dollars per provider, which the authors scaled to roughly seventy-eight million dollars per year across the country. They described the process as possibly predominantly administrative waste.

The cost per outlier detected has no finite value, because the denominator is zero. The Joint Commission itself concedes the underlying problem through its low-volume provision, which permits supplemental data from other facilities when local activity is too sparse to evaluate a provider. That carve-out is an admission written in policy language that the denominators are often too small to support the inference anyone wants to draw from them.

A working screen cannot beat a low base rate

The yield is empty in practice, and the reason is not only that the score is noisy. It is also that the thing the system is hunting for is rare, and rarity is unforgiving in a way no measurement effort repairs. Treat the OPPE flag as a screening test and read its operating characteristics off the same rule the funnel draws, flagging any provider with three or more events in thirty cases. Against a provider whose true rate is double, triple, or quadruple the base rate, that rule has a sensitivity of twenty-seven, fifty-one, and seventy-two percent, at a specificity of ninety-four percent. Those are respectable numbers for a cheap screen. The chart below shows what they are worth as the prevalence of dangerous providers changes.

The curves stay near the floor across the whole range a real base rate would occupy. At one percent prevalence, a flagged provider whose true rate is triple the norm is genuinely high-rate only about eight percent of the time, and the remaining ninety-two percent are unlucky rather than unsafe. Raising the sensitivity lifts the curves a little, raising the prevalence lifts them a lot, but prevalence is a property of the world rather than a dial anyone can turn. This is the base-rate fallacy in institutional dress. A screen with decent sensitivity and specificity, aimed at a rare condition, returns mostly false positives, and the people it flags are overwhelmingly ordinary.

Low yield is not zero value

Low yield is not zero value, and the distinction matters. A program that finds nothing through its metrics can still deter, can still catch the rare unambiguous case that no statistic was needed to see, and the entry-level work of verifying a license and screening for outright fraud does real good that has nothing to do with the reliability of a performance score. The claim worth making is narrow. Credentialing and the OPPE score have high face validity and weak predictive validity. They look like instruments that separate safe providers from dangerous ones, and at the volumes most physicians actually generate, they mostly cannot. Treating a noisy score as if it were a sharp one does more than waste money. It manufactures false confidence in both directions, flagging the unlucky and clearing no one in particular. The figures above are built on clearly labelled illustrative data, but the arithmetic they make visible is the same arithmetic that governs the real programs.

The three sourced figures in this post, the OPPE yield, the reliability sample sizes, and the quartile reclassification, are real and linked above. The funnel and the slopegraph are seeded simulations, generated by a stdlib-only script in this site's repo, and are not real provider data.
