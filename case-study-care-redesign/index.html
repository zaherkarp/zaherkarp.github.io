Did the care redesign work?

A primary care network rolled out a care-delivery redesign across thirty-some clinics. Eighteen months later, the question arrives. The way you answer it decides whether you get a real number or a flattering one.

The curves below are stylized to show how the method behaves, not real clinic data. The actual operational findings from this UW-Madison engagement were published with collaborators. This page is about the measurement design, not the results.

The tempting answer

Take the metric, average it for the year before the redesign, average it for the year after, and report the difference. The numbers exist. They tell a story. Here the post-period average sits well above the pre-period average, so the naive read is a large, clean win.

But the metric was already moving

The pre-versus-post comparison assumes the world stood still until the intervention. It did not. Fit a line to the pre-period and the metric is already drifting upward on its own: practice patterns change, patient mix shifts, payers introduce new measurement frameworks. That prior slope is the part the naive comparison silently attributes to the redesign.

Interrupted time series takes the prior trend seriously

Project the prior trend forward as the counterfactual: what the metric would likely have done with no intervention. The real effect is not the gap from the pre mean. It is the gap between what actually happened and that projection. A segmented regression splits it into two named pieces, the level shift the day the redesign goes live (β₂) and the slope change afterward (β₃).

The honest result is per metric, not one number

An intervention that changes clinic operations across several dimensions should not move every metric the same way, and this one did not. Reported honestly, the effect is a small grid: a one-time jump that then flattens on access, a slow build on documentation, and no movement at all on a third. Rolling these into a single composite would have destroyed exactly the signal the operational leads wanted to see.

What the design cannot tell you

Interrupted time series attributes the change at the intervention point to the intervention, conditional on the prior trend and seasonality. It cannot say which component of the redesign caused it, cannot fully separate the effect from a national event that happened to land at the same time, and cannot rescue a poorly defined metric. Knowing those limits is part of using the design well. The discipline that separates a credible attribution from a story dressed up in coefficients is the prior trend, the staggered-clinic structure, standard errors clustered at the clinic, and the autocorrelation check.
