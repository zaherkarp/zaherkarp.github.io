## Project Overview

During my time as a graduate student, I had the opportunity to lead a comprehensive research study investigating how physical clinic environments influence team interactions in family medicine settings. This project combined my interests in healthcare quality, human factors, and statistical analysis.

## Securing Funding

One of the most rewarding aspects of this project was successfully writing a grant proposal that secured $18,000 in funding. The grant writing process taught me how to:

- Clearly articulate research objectives and hypotheses
- Justify methodology and resource allocation
- Communicate the potential impact of research findings
- Navigate the administrative requirements of institutional funding

## Research Methodology

This was a true mixed-methods study, combining both qualitative observations and quantitative analysis:

### Qualitative Component

- **120 hours of direct observations** in family medicine clinics
- **9 focus groups** with 40 healthcare providers and staff
- Systematic coding and analysis of interaction patterns
- Documentation of environmental factors affecting workflow

### Quantitative Analysis

- Statistical analysis using SAS
- Measurement of communication frequency and quality
- Assessment of efficiency metrics
- Privacy perception surveys

## Key Findings

The research revealed several important insights about how clinic design affects healthcare delivery:

1. **Communication Patterns**: Open versus closed workspace designs significantly affected how often and how effectively team members communicated
2. **Efficiency Trade-offs**: Some design elements that improved privacy sometimes reduced operational efficiency
3. **Team Satisfaction**: Environmental factors had measurable effects on staff satisfaction and perceived quality of care

## IRB Protocol & Ethics

Navigating the Institutional Review Board (IRB) process was an essential part of this research. I developed protocols for:

## Publication & Impact

The research culminated in peer-reviewed publication, allowing the findings to reach a wider audience of healthcare administrators, architects, and policy makers. The study has been cited in discussions about evidence-based clinic design.
