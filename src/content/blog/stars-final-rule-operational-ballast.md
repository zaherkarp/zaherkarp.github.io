This is the first of two articles on the 2026 Medicare Advantage Star Ratings upheaval: this one covers the final rule, the measures it removes, and the contracts most exposed. The second covers the Clover Health ruling and the three-rulebook planning problem it creates for Stars analytics.

For years, operational and administrative measures were the quiet ballast of a Star Rating. Call center performance, complaint rates, appeals timeliness, member retention. Most plans scored well on them, year after year, and that reliable performance pulled contract averages up and damped volatility. They were a large part of the reason a contract with middling clinical results could still hold four stars.

The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) removed nearly all of them in the Contract Year 2027 final rule issued on April 2, 2026. The proposed rule had listed twelve measures for retirement; the final rule carried eleven of them through, sparing only the diabetic eye exam measure after commenters argued that retinal screening is a load-bearing piece of comprehensive diabetes care. The eleven break down as seven operational and administrative measures, two process measures, and two patient experience measures: timely appeals decisions, appeals review, the call center interpreter and TTY measures on both the Part C and Part D sides, plan complaints, Medicare Plan Finder accuracy, members choosing to leave the plan, Special Needs Plan care management, statin therapy for cardiovascular disease, and the CAHPS customer service and rating of health care quality measures. Three retire at Star Year 2028 and the remaining eight at Star Year 2029.

Two other decisions ride along. CMS will not implement the Excellent Health Outcomes for All reward, the renamed Health Equity Index reward, and will keep the historical reward factor instead, which cancels the new reward before its first scored year. And one measure arrives rather than departs: a Depression Screening and Follow-Up measure, the program's first behavioral health specific measure, enters at Star Year 2029 as a weight-one process measure after two display years.

## What survives is what is hardest to move

CMS's stated rationale for the retirements is that these measures score uniformly high and no longer differentiate contracts. That is true, and it is also exactly what made them valuable inside a plan. A measure everyone passes is a stabilizer. It contributes a reliable 4 or 5 stars to the weighted average every year, it absorbs bad luck elsewhere, and it rewards operational competence that plans directly control. Removing the undifferentiated measures is defensible program design and it is still the removal of ballast.

What remains is a program concentrated in the places plans find hardest to move. Press Ganey's analysis of the changes puts CAHPS and HOS at nearly 40 percent of total Star weight by 2029, and clinical HEDIS and Part D measures account for most of the remainder, approaching half of the overall rating. The changes already in flight make the concentration sharper. The two HOS improvement measures, notoriously difficult to manage because they measure change over time, move from a weight of one to a weight of three at Star Year 2027. The three Part D adherence measures step down to a weight of one at Star Year 2028 and back to three at Star Year 2029 while their methodology picks up sociodemographic risk adjustment. Two pharmacy safety measures join at Star Year 2027, concurrent use of opioids and benzodiazepines and polypharmacy with anticholinergics. And the transition of hybrid measures toward ECDS and electronic clinical quality measurement continues underneath all of it. The surface area for error grows while the cushion shrinks.

CMS's own impact estimate in the rule is calm: 62 percent of contracts see no change in overall rating, 13 percent gain half a star, 25 percent lose half a star, and one contract loses a full star. Independent modeling is less calm. Wakely's white paper on the rule projects the enrollment weighted market average overall rating falling roughly 0.24 stars between Star Year 2026 and Star Year 2029, from 4.02 to 3.78, with MA-PD payments declining about 1.2 percent across the market and all ten of the largest parent organizations losing revenue. Press Ganey, applying the removals to the 2026 results, finds 89 percent of contracts declining at least somewhat and about 1.3 billion dollars in quality bonus payments at stake.

The exposure is uneven in a specific direction. Contracts with heavy Special Needs Plan and dual eligible enrollment already run measurably below other contracts on average, and the operational measures being retired were the safety valve offsetting that gap. The same plans spent years building toward the health equity reward that will now never pay out. Smaller and midsize organizations carry a second disadvantage: thinner analytics teams, smaller quality budgets, and less provider engagement capacity to absorb a transition of this size.

## The playbook without the cushion

The old playbook assumed the ballast. The replacement has five parts. First, run a real impact assessment: score every contract under the future measure set and weights, identify which measures move from cushion to exposure, and measure how far each contract sits from the cut points that will decide its rating. Second, plan across multiple years, because a program dominated by clinical outcomes and member experience cannot be managed inside a single measurement cycle; HEDIS rates and survey scores move on provider contracting and member experience timelines measured in years. Third, invest in the levers that remain: clinical intervention programs, provider engagement, pharmacy programs, and member outreach are now the only instruments that reach the scores that count. Fourth, elevate CAHPS and HOS to enterprise priorities rather than survey season afterthoughts, since at nearly 40 percent of the weight they are no longer a category of the program but its center. Fifth, build a data strategy that survives the ECDS transition, which means clinical data integration and provider connectivity, not just claims.

The uncomfortable summary is that plans could once administer their way to four stars. That path closes on a published schedule between now and Star Year 2029. The remaining path runs through outcomes and experience, and the analytics job is to show precisely where each contract stands on that path and what closing the distance costs.
