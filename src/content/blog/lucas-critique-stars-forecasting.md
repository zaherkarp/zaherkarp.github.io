## The forecast that breaks on schedule

Every Medicare Advantage (MA) quality team maintains some version of the same artifact: a projection of next year's Star Ratings, built from historical cut points, historical measure scores, and historical relationships between investment and improvement. And every team that has maintained one for more than a few years has watched it fail in the same pattern. The forecast holds up tolerably in years when the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) leaves the methodology alone, then misses badly in years when the methodology changes. Which is to say: it misses in exactly the years when leadership most wants a number.

That pattern is not bad luck, and it is not a data problem you can fix with a better model of the same kind. It is a structural property of what these forecasts are. An economist named it fifty years ago.

## A 1976 paper about inflation

In 1976, Robert Lucas published [a critique of econometric policy evaluation](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0167-2231(76)80003-6) that reshaped macroeconomics. You need no macroeconomics to use it. The argument runs as follows: when you estimate a statistical relationship from historical data, the parameters you recover are not laws of nature. They are summaries of how people behaved under the rules that prevailed while the data was generated. People choose their behavior in response to the rules. Change the rules and people re-decide, which means the parameters you estimated are no longer descriptions of anything. In Lucas's words, "any change in policy will systematically alter the structure of econometric models."

The canonical casualty was the Phillips curve, a robust historical correlation between inflation and unemployment. Policymakers read the correlation as a menu: accept a little more inflation, buy a little less unemployment. When they tried to order from the menu, the correlation collapsed, because firms and workers adjusted their expectations to the new policy and the old tradeoff was a creature of the old expectations.

The [Wikipedia article](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucas_critique) carries a thought experiment that makes the logic vivid. Fort Knox has never been robbed. A naive statistical reading of that history says the guards accomplish nothing and can be cut. The error is obvious once stated: the zero-robbery record was generated by would-be robbers deciding, under the prevailing security regime, that robbery does not pay. Remove the guards and the decision changes. The historical parameter, robberies per year, was never a fact about Fort Knox. It was a fact about behavior under a regime.

Lucas's prescription was to model the things that survive a regime change, the deep parameters: preferences, constraints, capabilities. Predict behavior by re-deriving it under the new rules, rather than extrapolating the surface correlations the old rules produced. Readers who know [Goodhart's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodhart%27s_law), or Campbell's law, will recognize the family resemblance: those are the measurement world's versions of the same insight, that a measured relationship degrades once you build policy on top of it.

## Stars is a policy regime, and plans are the agents

The mapping onto Star Ratings is unusually clean. The CMS rule set, the measures, their weights, the cut point methodology, the relative scoring, is the policy regime. Plan behavior, where to spend improvement dollars, which measures to staff, which members to outreach, is the decision rule, and plans optimize it against the regime with considerable sophistication and several billion dollars of quality bonus payment (QBP) at stake. Any model trained on historical Stars data, whether a trend line or a gradient-boosted ensemble, is what economists call reduced-form: it captures the joint outcome of the rules and the behavior the rules induced, without separating the two.

That works fine while the regime holds. It is precisely the wrong tool the day the regime moves. Four exhibits, all public-knowledge policy facts.

## Exhibit one: cut points are equilibria, not trends

Cut points, the score thresholds that convert a measure rate into a measure star, are not set by CMS in advance. They are computed from the distribution of all plans' scores. They are, in other words, emergent properties of collective plan behavior under a given rule set, and plans target the thresholds they can see. A cut point series is therefore a series of equilibrium outcomes, and extrapolating it forward embeds the assumption that the behavior generating the equilibrium stays put.

The recent history of the methodology is a catalog of reasons it does not. Cut point guardrails, capping year-over-year movement at five percentage points for most measures, were finalized, delayed a year, and then took effect with the 2023 Star Ratings. Tukey outer-fence outlier deletion, which removes extreme low scores before cut points are computed, arrived with the 2024 Star Ratings and pulled the bottom thresholds up sharply. Each change redefined what the historical series even measures.

Look at this series the way a forecaster in late 2021 would have. Three years of near-perfect stability said the 4-star threshold lives at 76 percent. It then fell seven points in one year. Re-fit in 2023 and the bottom of the ladder said 42 to 43 percent; it then jumped nine points when Tukey deletion removed the low outliers that had been holding it down. Both moves were announced rule changes, not noise, and both were unforecastable from the series itself, because the series is downstream of the rules. A trend model fit across this history is averaging over three different games.

## Exhibit two: the 4x experiment

CMS raised the weight of the patient experience, complaints, and access measures, including the Consumer Assessment of Healthcare Providers and Systems (CAHPS) survey, from 2x to 4x beginning with the 2023 Star Ratings, then reduced them back to 2x beginning with the 2026 Star Ratings. Set aside whether either weighting was wise. Consider what the round trip does to any return-on-investment model for member experience programs.

A model calibrated during the 4x era learned a relationship between CAHPS investment and overall rating movement that was partly mechanical (the weights themselves) and partly behavioral (every plan reallocating toward the newly valuable measures, which shifts the relative-scoring landscape those measures are graded on). Both components were creatures of the regime. Under 2x, the mechanical component is half what the model learned, and the behavioral component is whatever the field's new allocation makes it. The historical coefficient is not conservative or optimistic; it is about a different world. The same logic runs prospectively: sociodemographic risk adjustment arrives on the Part D medication adherence measures with the 2026 measurement year, and their reduced interim weight changes the payoff structure for adherence work before a single new data point exists to model.

## Exhibit three: the tournament problem

Stars is graded on a curve. Cut points come from the distribution of competitors' scores, so your rating depends not on your performance but on your performance relative to a field that is also optimizing. This makes the most common projection exercise in the industry, "simulate our improvement plan against current cut points," a mechanical first-order estimate rather than a forecast. It answers: what happens if we alone respond to the rule change and every other plan stands still?

The error is easy to demonstrate without any proprietary data at all. I built a deliberately cartoonish tournament: 120 synthetic contracts, two generic measures, a fixed effort budget per contract with diminishing returns, star thresholds at fixed percentiles of the field recomputed each round, and contracts that best-respond to last year's thresholds. Then I raised the weight on the second measure and computed each contract's projection two ways. The mechanical way re-optimizes that contract alone, freezes all competitors, and recomputes the thresholds. The equilibrium way lets every contract re-optimize at once, because they can all read the Federal Register.

The numbers are synthetic; the mechanic is not. Relative scoring means the field's response is not a refinement of your projection. It is most of the answer. A projection that freezes competitors is not a cautious version of the truth, it is a different quantity, and it should be labeled as one.

## Exhibit four: the pandemic years are a different regime

For the 2021 and 2022 Star Ratings, CMS applied extreme and uncontrollable circumstances provisions, including a "higher of" rule that let most measures revert to the prior year's rating if performance fell. The result is visible in the published distributions: roughly 49 percent of MA contracts with prescription drug coverage earned 4 or more stars in the 2021 Star Ratings, then about 68 percent in 2022, falling back to about 51 percent in 2023 and roughly 44 and 41 percent in the two years after, as the provisions expired and Tukey arrived. CMS itself attributed the 2022 bulge to nearly all contracts qualifying for the adjustments.

A robust-statistics instinct says: downweight 2022 as an outlier year. The Lucas framing is sharper and more useful. Those ratings were not the usual process plus a shock. They were generated by different rules, and by plan behavior optimized for those rules, with relief provisions changing what was worth measuring and managing. An outlier is a draw from your model's tails. A regime change is a draw from someone else's model. Robust estimators shrink the first kind of contamination; only explicit handling, exclusion, re-derivation, or modeling the rule directly, addresses the second.

## What to do differently

None of this says quantitative Stars work is futile. It says the honest unit of work is different from the one most forecasts ship with. Concretely:

- Label every projection as either mechanical recomputation (new rules applied to frozen behavior, including your own) or behavioral forecast (new rules plus a model of the response, yours and the field's), and attach the much wider uncertainty the second deserves. Most deliverables silently blend the two.
- When a projection crosses a rule boundary, prefer member-level structural simulation, recomputing measures from eligibility and event data under the new specification, over contract-level trend extrapolation. The member-level data generating process survives rule changes; the contract-level correlations do not.
- Treat announced methodology changes as structural breaks. Do not train across them without explicit handling, and in the first year after a change, present scenario ranges rather than point estimates, because the field's behavioral response is not yet in any data you have.
- Watch for the internal version of Goodhart's law: any operational dashboard or KPI pinned to a Stars measure will, over time, distort what that measure tells you about underlying care, for exactly the reasons above, with your own organization as the optimizing agent.

## Model the deep parameters

Lucas's positive prescription translates directly. The things that survive a CMS rule change are the deep parameters of your plan: the member population and its clinical reality, the provider network, the operational capacity to close gaps, the cost of moving each measure by a point. Last year's cut point trend is not on the list, and neither is the historical correlation between any investment and any star. Model the capabilities, re-derive the behavior under the new rules, and recompute the outcome. That is what separating mechanical recomputation from behavioral forecasting means, and it is the difference between a forecast that degrades politely and one that fails, on schedule, the day the Federal Register changes the game everyone is playing.
