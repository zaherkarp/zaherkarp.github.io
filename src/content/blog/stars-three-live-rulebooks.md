This is the second of two articles on the 2026 Medicare Advantage Star Ratings upheaval. Part 1 covered the final rule the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) issued in April: eleven operational and administrative measures retire by Star Year 2029, and the weight they carried shifts onto the clinical HEDIS, Part D, CAHPS, and HOS scores that plans find hardest to move. This part is about what landed on top of that rule, and what it does to every projection a Stars team maintains.

On May 27, 2026, the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Georgia ruled for Clover Health in its challenge to the 2026 Star Ratings. The posture matters as much as the outcome. Clover did not contest a cut point or a calculation; it contested CMS's authority to include entire measures in the rating at all. That argument became available in its current strength when the Supreme Court's Loper Bright decision ended Chevron deference in 2024 and put statutory interpretation back with the courts. The district court read the Medicare statute as authorizing ratings built from the data types it names, in practice HEDIS, HOS, and CAHPS, and held that roughly twenty measures were unlawful: ten because they rest on other data sources, such as call center monitoring, appeals records, and prescription drug event feeds, and ten more because CMS failed notice and comment rulemaking when adopting them [VERIFY]. The court set aside Clover's 2026 Star Rating, and on recalculation the contract moved from 3.5 to 4.5 stars [VERIFY].

The ruling did not stay contained to one plan. In June, CMS announced it would voluntarily recalculate ratings for every Medicare Advantage organization using only the authorized data categories, holding harmless any contract that would score lower under the narrower set. CMS has reserved the right to appeal, Congress may eventually intervene given how much bonus payment money rides on the program, and the decision could still be stayed or narrowed. None of that has resolved.

## Three rulebooks, one program

A Stars team is therefore planning against three rulebooks at once. The first is the current program on its published glidepath: the measure retirements landing at Star Years 2028 and 2029, the HOS improvement measures moving to triple weight, the Part D adherence weight changes, the new pharmacy safety measures. The second is the destination state, CMS's streamlined Star Year 2029 program of roughly 34 measures [VERIFY]. The third is the Clover shape: a program restricted to HEDIS, HOS, and CAHPS, somewhere between 16 and 19 measures [VERIFY], a deeper cut than CMS's own reform. The third rulebook is not hypothetical. The recalculation is being applied to 2026 ratings now, as a hold harmless overlay on scores that plans have already banked.

These are not three variations on one program. Each implies a different measure list, different weights, a different contract population feeding cut point clustering, different reward factor eligibility, and different categorical adjustment index groupings. Cut points are the clearest casualty. They come from clustering the observed distribution of scores, and the clusters depend on which measures exist and which contracts sit in the pool. Remove a third to a half of the measure set and every distribution shifts, which means the cut point distances your remediation plan was built on are artifacts of a rulebook that may not govern the year the plan pays off. A projection built under one rulebook tells you nothing reliable about your position under the other two, and averaging across them is worse: a contract can be comfortable under one rulebook and badly exposed under another, and the average describes neither situation. The hold harmless recalculation adds a further wrinkle, because for the 2026 rating year there are now effectively two official scores for the same contract, and downstream planning has to know which one it is looking at.

## The rule layer as configuration

The operational answer is to stop treating the rule layer as code and start treating it as configuration. Most Stars pipelines hard-code the current program: the measure list lives in the queries, the weights live in the scoring step, the eligibility and exclusion logic lives wherever it accreted. That design assumes one rulebook. The alternative is to make measure lists, weights, eligibility flags, exclusion rules, and reward factor and adjustment parameters versioned data, so that the same scoring engine can run under the current program, the streamlined 2029 program, or a Clover style program by pointing it at a different configuration version. Every projection run gets tagged with the scenario it ran under. Exposure reporting then shows the same contract side by side under all three rulebooks, so leadership sees the range of outcomes rather than a single number that may be obsolete by the time the appeal resolves. A Stars number without its rulebook version attached is not a forecast; it is a guess with a timestamp.

This is real work, and it is tempting to defer it until one future wins. The problem is timing. Whichever rulebook prevails, the plans that modeled it in advance will already know their measure gaps, their cut point distances, and where to spend, and they will have had that knowledge during the years when interventions still had time to move a rate. The plans that waited will spend the first year of the new regime doing the impact assessment everyone else finished during the uncertainty. Uncertainty is expensive, but it is cheapest for the teams that built for it.
